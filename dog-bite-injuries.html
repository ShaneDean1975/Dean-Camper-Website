Dog bites and animal attacks cause serious injuries, especially to children. Dog Bite Statistics

  • 4.5 million dog bites occur annually in the United States
  • Children are the most common victims of dog attacks
  • Face and neck injuries are most common in children
  • Homeowner's insurance pays over $1 billion annually in dog bite claims
  • Average dog bite settlement exceeds $50,000

Florida Dog Bite Law - Strict Liability

Florida Statute 767.04 makes Florida one of the most victim-friendly states for dog bite cases:

  • Dog Owner is Liable - Regardless of the dog's prior behavior or history
  • No "One Free Bite" Rule - Even first-time bites create automatic liability
  • No Need to Prove Negligence - Strict liability statute eliminates this burden
  • Exceptions Apply For: Trespassing victims, provocation by victim
  • Comparative Negligence - Victim's fault may reduce but not eliminate recovery

Alabama Dog Bite Law - Scienter (One-Bite Rule)

Alabama follows the common law "one-bite rule," which is less favorable to victims:

  • Must Prove Knowledge - Owner knew or should have known dog was dangerous
  • Prior Bites Establish Knowledge - Previous attacks prove dangerous propensity
  • Aggressive Behavior Counts - Growling, lunging, prior aggressive incidents
  • Breed Reputation May Help - Dangerous breed characteristics can establish knowledge
  • Alternative: Negligence - Can prove owner failed to properly restrain dog
  • Alternative: Premises Liability - Dangerous condition on property theory

Common Dog Bite Injuries

  • Puncture Wounds and Lacerations - Deep bites requiring stitches or surgery
  • Facial Scarring and Disfigurement - Permanent visible scars, especially in children
  • Eye Injuries and Vision Loss - Serious facial attacks affecting eyes
  • Broken Bones - From knock-downs or the force of the bite
  • Nerve Damage - Loss of sensation or motor function
  • Infections - Including serious infections and rabies risk
  • Tendon and Muscle Damage - Requiring reconstructive surgery
  • Emotional Trauma and PTSD - Long-lasting psychological effects
  • Reconstructive Surgery Needs - Multiple surgeries for severe injuries
  • Permanent Scarring - Disfigurement requiring ongoing treatment

Children as Dog Bite Victims

Children are especially vulnerable to dog attacks:

  • Face and Head Injuries Common - Children's faces are at dog's level
  • Long-Term Psychological Trauma - Fear of dogs and animals persisting into adulthood
  • Permanent Scarring on Growing Bodies - Scars become more prominent as child grows
  • Fear and Anxiety Disorders - PTSD and phobias developing after attacks
  • Developmental Impacts - Social and emotional development affected

Courts and juries are particularly sympathetic to child victims, understanding the lifelong impact of dog attacks.

Dangerous Dog Breeds

While any dog can bite, certain breeds are statistically more dangerous:

  • Pit Bulls - Responsible for the majority of fatal dog attacks
  • Rottweilers - Large, powerful dogs with protective instincts
  • German Shepherds - Strong bite force, territorial behavior
  • Huskies - High prey drive, can be unpredictable
  • Doberman Pinschers - Protective, can be aggressive
  • Mastiffs - Massive size and strength
  • Chows - Known for aggressive tendencies

Important: While breed matters in evaluating danger, owner responsibility is the key legal issue in liability cases.

When Dog Owners Are Liable

Dog owners can be held responsible when:

  • Dog bites or attacks anyone lawfully on their property
  • Dog escapes and attacks someone off the property
  • Dog shows signs of aggression and owner fails to control it
  • Owner violates local leash laws
  • Owner knows dog is dangerous and fails to secure it properly
  • Dog attacks postal workers, delivery drivers, or invited guests

Defenses Dog Owners Use

Dog owners and their insurance companies will try to avoid liability:

  • Victim Was Trespassing - Claiming no legal right to be on property
  • Victim Provoked the Dog - Alleging victim teased or hurt the dog
  • Comparative Negligence - Blaming victim's actions for the attack
  • No Prior Knowledge (Alabama) - Claiming dog never showed aggression before
  • "Beware of Dog" Sign Posted - May reduce but doesn't eliminate liability

We know how to overcome these defenses with proper evidence and legal arguments.

Homeowner's Insurance Coverage

Most dog bite claims are paid by the dog owner's homeowner's insurance:

  • Typical Coverage - Policies usually cover $100,000-$300,000 for liability
  • Breed Exclusions - Many insurers exclude certain dangerous breeds
  • Post-Bite Coverage - Some insurers cancel coverage after one bite incident
  • We Handle Insurance Companies - Direct negotiation with adjusters on your behalf

Compensation Available in Dog Bite Cases

  • Medical Expenses - Emergency care, surgery, antibiotics, ongoing treatment
  • Future Medical Costs - Plastic surgery, scar revision, reconstructive procedures
  • Plastic Surgery - Multiple procedures for facial reconstruction and scar reduction
  • Pain and Suffering - Physical pain from injuries and treatment
  • Emotional Distress and PTSD - Psychological trauma and counseling needs
  • Permanent Scarring and Disfigurement - Compensation for visible permanent injuries
  • Lost Wages - If unable to work during recovery
  • Counseling and Therapy - Psychological treatment for trauma

Proving Damages in Dog Bite Cases

Strong evidence is essential to maximize compensation:

  • Medical Records and Bills - All treatment documentation
  • Photos of Injuries - Immediately after attack and during healing process
  • Expert Testimony - Plastic surgeons on permanent scarring and treatment needs
  • Psychological Evaluation - Mental health professionals documenting PTSD
  • Before and After Photos - Showing appearance change from scarring
  • Witness Statements - From those who saw the attack
  • Economic Damages - Documentation of lost wages and expenses

Reporting Dog Bites

Proper reporting protects you and others:

  • Report to Local Animal Control - Creates official record of attack
  • Report to Police - Criminal report may be appropriate
  • Report to Health Department - Rabies risk assessment and quarantine
  • Get Dog Owner Information - Contact details and homeowner's insurance
  • Document the Dog - Photos, breed, size, identifying characteristics

Official reports protect others from future attacks and strengthen your legal case.

Statute of Limitations

Florida: 2 years from the date of the dog bite attack.

Alabama: 2 years from the date of the dog bite attack.

Don't delay. Evidence can disappear, witnesses forget details, and the dog owner may move or change insurance. Contact us immediately after a dog attack.

Rabies Concerns

If the dog's vaccination status is unknown:

  • Victim may need rabies post-exposure prophylaxis (series of painful shots)
  • Dog must be quarantined and observed for rabies symptoms
  • Owner is liable for unnecessary treatment costs if vaccination status unclear
  • Failure to vaccinate dog may increase owner's liability

Leash Laws

Most Florida and Alabama municipalities have leash laws:

  • Dogs must be on leash in public spaces
  • Violations of leash laws establish negligence
  • Even "friendly" dogs must be leashed per local ordinances
  • Leash law violations strengthen your liability case

What to Do After a Dog Bite

  • 1. Seek Immediate Medical Attention - Even minor bites can become infected
  • 2. Clean Wound Thoroughly - Reduces infection risk before medical care
  • 3. Document Injuries - Photos from multiple angles, close-ups of wounds
  • 4. Get Dog Owner Information - Name, address, phone, homeowner's insurance, vaccination records
  • 5. Report to Authorities - Animal control, police, health department
  • 6. Keep All Medical Records - Bills, treatment notes, prescriptions, future care recommendations
  • 7. Don't Sign Anything - Owner or insurance company may pressure you
  • 8. Contact Attorney - Before giving statements to insurance adjusters
